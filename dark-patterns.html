Subscription Practices

The Dark Patterns Making It Hard to Cancel

Consumer Rights Commission · 2026 · ← Back to Articles

Signing up for a subscription service typically takes under a minute. Cancelling one can require navigating multiple confirmation screens, enduring retention offers, finding a cancellation option buried in account settings, or calling a phone number during business hours. This asymmetry is not accidental — it is the product of deliberate interface design intended to reduce cancellations at the expense of consumer experience.

What Dark Patterns Are

Dark patterns are user interface design choices that manipulate users into taking actions they did not intend or would not take if the interface were neutral. The term was coined by UX designer Harry Brignull in 2010 and has since been adopted in regulatory and academic contexts. In the subscription context, dark patterns typically serve to increase sign-ups, reduce cancellations, and obscure pricing.

Common documented patterns include: roach motels (easy in, difficult out), confirmshaming (cancellation buttons labeled with emotionally loaded language like "No thanks, I don't want to save money"), hidden costs revealed only at checkout, and forced continuity — where free trials automatically convert to paid subscriptions without adequate notice.

Documented Examples

The US Federal Trade Commission has taken enforcement action against several companies for subscription practices. Amazon's Prime cancellation flow was the subject of a 2023 FTC complaint alleging that the company used manipulative design to prevent cancellations, including a multi-step process that the FTC described as intentionally difficult. Amazon subsequently simplified the cancellation process following regulatory pressure.

Consumer reporting organizations have documented similar patterns across streaming services, gym memberships, software subscriptions, and news outlets. The pattern is sufficiently widespread that researchers at Princeton University developed an automated detection system capable of identifying dark patterns at scale across large numbers of websites.

Regulatory Response

Regulators in multiple jurisdictions have begun addressing dark patterns explicitly. The FTC's 2023 "click-to-cancel" rule requires that cancellation be made as easy as sign-up. The EU's Digital Services Act and Consumer Rights Directive include provisions addressing manipulative design. The UK's Competition and Markets Authority has issued guidance and taken enforcement action in this area.

Enforcement remains inconsistent, and the regulatory frameworks in place have not eliminated the practices. Consumer awareness and reporting remain important mechanisms for identifying and addressing new instances.

What Consumers Can Do

Consumers who encounter manipulative cancellation flows can report them to their national consumer protection authority. Documenting the experience with screenshots provides useful evidence. The Commission recommends consumers use virtual credit cards with spending limits for trial subscriptions and maintain records of subscription sign-up dates and terms.
