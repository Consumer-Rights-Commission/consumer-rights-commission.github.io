Data Privacy

What Your Apps Know About You — And Who They're Telling

Consumer Rights Commission · 2026 · ← Back to Articles

The modern smartphone is a continuous data collection instrument. Applications installed on consumer devices routinely collect location data, browsing behavior, purchase history, health indicators, and social connections — and a significant portion of that data is sold or shared with third parties through a largely invisible ecosystem of data brokers.

What Gets Collected

App permissions requested at install time represent only a fraction of the data collected. SDKs — software development kits embedded in apps by third-party analytics and advertising companies — collect data independently of the app's stated functionality. A weather app that requests location access may transmit that location data to dozens of advertising partners the consumer has never heard of and whose privacy policies they have never read.

Categories of commonly collected data include precise GPS location, device identifiers, contact lists, microphone and camera usage patterns, and behavioral data derived from how consumers interact with their devices. Aggregated, this data allows brokers to construct detailed profiles of individuals.

The Data Broker Ecosystem

Data brokers are companies whose business model is the collection, aggregation, and sale of consumer data. The industry is large, largely unregulated in most jurisdictions, and operates with minimal consumer visibility. Consumers typically have no direct relationship with the brokers who hold their data and limited ability to access or correct it.

Documented purchasers of data broker products include insurance companies, employers, law enforcement agencies, and political campaigns. The uses to which purchased data is put are not disclosed to the consumers whose data is involved.

What the Law Currently Provides

Consumer data protection varies significantly by jurisdiction. The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation provides relatively strong protections including rights of access, correction, and deletion. In the United States, federal consumer data protection remains limited, with protections varying by state. California's Consumer Privacy Act represents one of the stronger US frameworks.

In most jurisdictions, the consent frameworks through which data collection is nominally authorized — lengthy terms of service and privacy policies — have been found in research studies to be practically unreadable and functionally not meaningful consent.

Practical Steps for Consumers

Consumers can reduce their exposure by reviewing and limiting app permissions, using browsers with tracking protection, opting out of data broker profiles where legally permitted, and choosing services with stronger stated privacy practices. The Commission recommends consumers treat data minimization as a standard practice rather than an exceptional one.
