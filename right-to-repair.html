Right to Repair

Why Manufacturers Don't Want You Fixing Your Own Device

Consumer Rights Commission · 2026 · ← Back to Articles

When a consumer purchases a device — a smartphone, a laptop, a tractor — the expectation is that ownership includes the right to maintain and repair that device. Manufacturers have increasingly moved to restrict that right through a combination of software locks, proprietary parts, and warranty policies designed to funnel repair work back to authorized channels.

How Repair Restrictions Work

Modern devices are engineered with barriers to independent repair. These include serialized components that require manufacturer software to pair after replacement, warranty terms that void coverage upon third-party repair, and the deliberate withholding of diagnostic tools and parts from independent technicians.

Apple's "parts pairing" system, for example, means that replacing a genuine Apple battery with another genuine Apple battery — if the transaction occurs outside an Apple-authorized facility — can trigger software warnings and reduced functionality. The component itself is not defective; the restriction is enforced by software.

The Consumer Cost

Repair restrictions translate directly into higher costs for consumers. When independent repair is effectively blocked, consumers face a choice between paying manufacturer or authorized-dealer rates — which are documented to be significantly higher — or replacing the device entirely. Research from organizations including iFixit and the US Public Interest Research Group has consistently found that repair restrictions increase the total cost of device ownership.

The environmental impact compounds the economic one. Devices discarded because repair is economically or practically inaccessible contribute to a growing e-waste stream. The United Nations estimates tens of millions of tonnes of e-waste are generated annually, a figure that repair access would meaningfully reduce.

Legislative Response

A growing number of jurisdictions have moved to legislate repair access. The European Union's Ecodesign Regulation requires manufacturers of certain product categories to make spare parts and repair information available. Several US states have passed or introduced Right to Repair legislation, with Minnesota and Colorado among the early adopters of meaningful consumer repair protections.

Manufacturer lobbying against these measures has been substantial and well-documented, with industry groups arguing — without consistent evidentiary support — that repair access creates safety risks and intellectual property concerns.

What Consumers Can Do

Consumers who wish to support Right to Repair can contact their elected representatives in support of relevant legislation, prioritize purchasing devices with repairability ratings from organizations like iFixit, and support independent repair businesses. The Commission recommends members consider these actions as evidence-based forms of consumer pressure.
